Strategy 6 : Landscape Restoration

Development has impacts on the environment. The development of different sectors in South Sumatra also has impacts on the functions of the environment, including land degradation, particularly by land-based development such as the development of plantation forests, mines, agricultural land and plantations. Moreover, land is also being degraded by forest and land fires, which have repeatedly occurred for the last several years in this province. Restoration needs to be carried out to restore and improve the functions of the environment that have lost, particularly in order to tackle land degradation issues as an impact of development.

Intervention 6.1 Financing, policies and partnerships that support restoration programs

Development in South Sumatra has impacts in that some of environmental functions are lost and lands are degraded. Environmental degradation is inevitable in development. To recover and improve environmental functions that have been degraded, restoration is needed. Restoration needs a considerable amount of money, and therefore a strong commitment is needed from the government to make policies to allocate government funds for restoration activities. Furthermore, partnerships and cooperation at the local, national and international levels also need to be developed to open up opportunities to finance restoration. In addition, restoration that has been done by community members also needs to be identified and documented.

Intervention 6.2 Land restoration using the agroforestry system (local coffee and rubber)

One way to restore degraded land in South Sumatra is agroforestry, which combines farm crops and trees. Rubber and coffee are two key commodities in South Sumatra, which can be cultivated using agroforestry. With agroforestry, degraded land will be able to be restored while communities’ income is improved.

Intervention 6.3 Replanting industrial timber plantations in primary crop zones

Industrial timber plantations should be sustainably managed to make them productive and to avoid environmental degradation. Replanting industrial timber plantations, particularly the crops for livelihoods zones, is significant by taking the silviculture principles into account and conducting the monitoring of every growing period.

Intervention 6.4 Rehabilitation (replanting) in crops for livelihoods zones

The share of a crops for livelihoods zone is 20 percent of the total area of an industrial timber plantation managed in partnership with community members living around the industrial timber plantation. The restoration of these zones should involve the locals to protect the zones from either natural disasters such as fires or encroachment. This can be done by establishing a forest protection program with the community. Additionally, potential for ecotourism should be explore as well in order to protect crops for livelihoods zones and at the same time improve the locals’ livelihoods.

Intervention 6.5 Natural succession of HCV and HCS areas

HCV and HCS areas are not free from the threats of environmental and land degradation. The functions and quality of these areas should immediately be restored. Natural succession is a process in which gradual changes happen over time to a degraded ecosystem. For natural succession to happen, assurance is needed that the environment is protected from external disturbances, including encroachment and fires. Succession area safeguarding should be a priority, together with public awareness raising about the importance of HCV and HCS area restoration.

Intervention 6.6 Rehabilitation of degraded HCV and HCS areas

In some degraded HCV and HCS areas, land rehabilitation needs to be conducted through planting, particularly planting local species suitable for the local condition. The rehabilitation should be supported with assisted regeneration, and the progress should be monitored to ensure it is well implemented and the success indicators are achieved.

Intervention 6.7 Peatland restoration

Many peatlands in South Sumatra have been burned in the last several years, particularly in 2015 (add more data). Peatlands are crucial ecosystems and sequestrate a large amount of carbon (how much). Peatlands in South Sumatra need to be restored in order to improve their degraded condition and to avoid fires in the future. Peatlands that the community has worked on (the cultivation zones) need a farming system suitable for peatlands without needing any drainage systems, which is known as paludiculture.

Intervention 6.8 Integrated forest and land fire management

Forest and land fires in South Sumatra need attention from everyone. The involvement and participation of the community, the private sector, NGOs and the government to tackle fires are critical. The community needs to be empowered and educated about fire prevention, for example through training about zero-burning farming methods, the provision of knowledge about early detection and fire suppression. NGOs and the private sector can be the government’s partners for community empowerment. The government should ne firm when making policies and taking actions against fires. If everyone actively works hand in hand to tackle this problem, the possibility for forest and land fire management to succeed will be bigger.
